How floor votes break down, by chamber

share of final-passage votes, House vs. Senate

We classified every House- or Senate-wide final-passage roll call this session into three buckets: unanimous (no recorded opposition), bipartisan-split (opposition exists but doesn't divide along party lines), and party-line (a majority of Republicans opposed a majority of Democrats, or vice versa). Both chambers land on party lines about as often — but the Senate is far more likely to be unanimous, while the House sees more genuine bipartisan disagreement that still isn't a party split.

Most party-splitting votes

by total crossover votes, both parties combined

Ranked by total members voting against their own party's majority. Property tax and school-funding bills dominate this list — the one recurring subject where Ohio Democrats split internally rather than voting as a bloc, since the impact varies sharply by district.

The single biggest crossover vote, HB129 (limiting school districts' ability to reallocate property tax millage), split House Democrats 19–14 while Republicans held together 62–2 — a pattern that repeats across several property-tax and school-funding bills this session, including HB309, HB186, and HB57.

Methodology. We pulled every bill LegiScan marked as passed/enacted (status 4) in the 136th General Assembly's 2025–2026 regular session — 1,007 bills — via the LegiScan API's getBill operation, then extracted all 577 unique roll calls referenced across them. Of those, 261 were House- or Senate-wide final-passage or adoption votes with at least 10 recorded participants; committee votes, procedural motions, and re-referrals were excluded. Each roll call's per-member votes (getRollCall) were joined against the session's full member roster (getSessionPeople) to attach party affiliation. A vote is "party-line" when a majority of voting Republicans and a majority of voting Democrats landed on opposite sides; a member's "crossover" is any Yea/Nay vote against their own party's majority position on that roll call. Member unity rates require at least 20 qualifying votes.

This is a single-session snapshot of the 136th General Assembly (through July 2026), not a multi-session trend — a bill with no recorded roll call (voice vote, unanimous consent, or a version LegiScan didn't capture a division on) isn't represented here, which is why only 159 of 1,007 passed bills contributed a roll call at all. Independent and vacant seats are excluded from party-majority calculations.
