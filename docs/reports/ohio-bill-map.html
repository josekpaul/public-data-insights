Legislative Data Brief

Who writes Ohio's laws, and what they actually do

Two out of every five items filed in Columbus is a ceremonial resolution, not a law. Of the roughly 1,400 real bills introduced this General Assembly, only 7% pass — and the ones that do mostly patch narrow rules for specific professions and agencies, not sweeping policy change.

What actually becomes law

100 real bills passed this session, classified by who they affect

LegiScan doesn't tag Ohio bills by subject, so we read all 100 enacted bills' titles and summaries by hand. Most passed legislation is narrow and technical — a professional licensing tweak, a cross-reference fix, a single county's tax mechanism — not broad policy change. The two budget bills (operating and capital) are the exception: they're the only items that touch the whole state at once.

How these bills act

mechanism of the 100 passed bills

Beyond who's affected, most passed bills fall into one of four types of action. Sweeping regulatory change is rare — even bills with long titles are usually amending a handful of existing code sections, not building new regulatory regimes from scratch.

The clearest exceptions — bills that build genuinely new regulatory structures rather than patching old ones — are HB170 (carbon capture and storage, an entirely new permitting regime), SB56 (marijuana law and tax overhaul), and HB105 (litigation funding, a wholly new chapter of code). Everything else is closer to maintenance than reinvention.

Methodology. Bill counts and sponsor data come from the LegiScan API's getBill operation, pulled for all 2,443 items (bills and resolutions) filed in the 136th General Assembly's 2025–2026 regular session. "Real bills" excludes House and Senate Resolutions (HR/SR/HCR/SCR/HJR/SJR), which require no committee vote to reach the floor and pass nearly automatically. District boundaries are the Census Bureau's 2024 TIGER/Line State Legislative District (Lower Chamber) shapefile for Ohio, simplified for web display; each bill is attributed to its prime sponsor's district, so co-sponsorships are not counted. The "what actually becomes law" and "how these bills act" classifications were built by manually reading each of the 100 enacted bills' LSC-drafted title and summary text — a judgment call, not a keyword match, since LegiScan does not supply subject tags for Ohio bills.

This report reflects the 136th General Assembly as of July 2026; some items LegiScan lists with 2026 action dates several months in the future appear to be data-entry artifacts in the source and were not used to determine "recent" activity.
