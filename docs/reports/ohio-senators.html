Ohio's senators, two very different money stories

Bernie Moreno and Jon Husted vote together almost every time — the real contrast shows up in fundraising, and in what Moreno's own trading disclosures reveal about a bill he sponsored.

Party-unity rate

share of sampled votes with Republican majority

Across a sample of 220 roll-call votes spanning both sessions of this Congress, Moreno and Husted agreed with the Senate Republican majority position 99.5% of the time each — a tighter margin than any Ohio House Republican in our companion brief. Both senators broke from the GOP majority on exactly one sampled vote, and it was the same one: a Rand Paul amendment to the FY2025 budget resolution, where the Republican conference itself split narrowly and Paul's position lost.

Republican

The one break for both: S.Amdt. 999, Rand Paul's amendment to the FY2025 budget resolution (S.Con.Res. 7), rejected 24–76 on Feb 21, 2025. Moreno and Husted joined Paul's minority fiscal-hawk faction rather than the broader Senate GOP position — an intra-party split, not a break toward Democrats.

Legislative activity

bills and resolutions, this Congress

Husted has introduced substantially more legislation than Moreno despite comparable tenure — 42 sponsored bills to Moreno's 26, plus nearly three times the amendments. Neither senator has had a sponsored bill signed into law yet, typical for first-term senators this early in a Congress. Cosponsorship tells a similar story: Moreno has cosponsored more measures relative to his own sponsorship activity, suggesting a delegation-support role on colleagues' bills alongside his own agenda.

Among Moreno's sponsored measures: S.Res. 708, a resolution to amend Senate rules and prohibit senators from trading on prediction markets, agreed to by unanimous consent in April 2026. His own Periodic Transaction Reports (below) show a $100,001–$250,000 sale of eToro Group stock the following month — eToro is a trading platform, not a prediction market, so the resolution doesn't cover it, but the juxtaposition is notable given his broader pattern of trading through that same platform.

Stock trading activity

Periodic Transaction Reports, this Congress

Moreno has filed 7 disclosed transactions since taking office; Husted's spouse has filed one. Moreno's trading is unusual for the pattern we've seen in other delegations: rather than plain equities, most of his purchases are bank-issued structured notes — "autocallable contingent yield notes" from JPMorgan, Bank of America, Goldman Sachs, and a Canadian bank — a complex derivative product tied to index performance. He also disclosed a Bitcoin sale and, five months later, a much larger sale of eToro Group stock, the crypto/stock trading platform where that Bitcoin sale took place.

Husted's only disclosed transaction is a spouse's $1,001–$15,000 purchase of Series A-3 preferred stock in Boxabl, a private Las Vegas-based home-construction startup — a non-public holding, not a tradeable security.

Trades vs. Banking Committee activity

Moreno only — sits on Senate Banking, Housing & Urban Affairs

Moreno sits on the Senate Banking Committee, and several of his structured-note purchases are issued by major banks the committee oversees. We checked whether his 7 trade dates cluster near Banking Committee hearing dates more than chance would predict. They do, but only mildly — not enough to draw a conclusion from.

Base rate check: given how often the Banking Committee meets (roughly every two weeks throughout Moreno's term), a randomly chosen date lands within 6 days of some hearing about 54% of the time by chance alone — a 10,000-trial simulation over his full tenure. Moreno's actual trades landed within 6 days 5 of 7 times (71%), and within 1 day 3 of 7 times (43%, vs. a 14% base rate for a random date). Both are elevated, but with only 7 trades total, this sample is too small to distinguish a real pattern from coincidence — it would take a larger disclosure history, or a look at his actual committee vote/hearing participation (not just hearing dates), to say more.

Campaign finance

current cycle, as of latest FEC filing

Husted's committee has raised more than five times what Moreno's has this cycle — $14.35M to $2.73M — and carries zero committee debt with $9.4M cash on hand. Moreno's committee, by contrast, still owes $7.04M, almost entirely self-loans from his expensive 2024 general election that he's repaying with current fundraising. Husted also draws a substantially higher individual-donor share (43.7% vs. 26.6%), meaning more of Moreno's money comes from PACs and party committees relative to individuals.

Methodology. Congress.gov's API does not cover Senate roll-call votes, so voting data here is pulled directly from senate.gov's public roll-call XML feed. Rather than fetch all 857 votes across both sessions of this Congress, we sampled every third vote (220 of 286 sampled votes returned valid member-level detail) and computed each senator's agreement rate with the Republican conference's majority position on each vote — this is an estimate, not an exhaustive count, and differs from the House party-unity brief's full 110-vote contested sample. Legislative activity is pulled via Congress.gov's /member/{bioguideId}/sponsored-legislation and /cosponsored-legislation endpoints. Stock-trade data comes directly from efdsearch.senate.gov's Periodic Transaction Report filings — unlike House PTRs, Senate reports are published as structured HTML rather than PDF, so no text-extraction was needed. Campaign finance figures are FEC principal-committee totals for the current election cycle (selected as the committee with the most recent active cycle, since both senators have more than one registered committee). Banking Committee hearing dates are pulled from banking.senate.gov's public hearings calendar; the base rate for the trades-vs-hearings check is a 10,000-trial Monte Carlo simulation of random dates across Moreno's full tenure (Jan 3, 2025 – Jul 16, 2026) against the actual hearing calendar.

This is a first-pass exploratory read, not a definitive study. The sampled-vote approach means the 99.5% party-unity figures are estimates with a margin of error; a full census of all 857 votes could shift them slightly. Both senators took office within the past two years, so "performance" here reflects an early, still-developing track record rather than a full term's body of work.
